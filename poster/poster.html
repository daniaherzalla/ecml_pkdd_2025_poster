Graph Neural Networks for Jamming Source Localization

Dania Herzalla¹, Willian T. Lunardi¹, Martin Andreoni¹²
¹Technology Innovation Institute, Abu Dhabi, UAE    ²Khalifa University

Introduction

Problem: Jamming attacks threaten wireless networks by transmitting intentional interference, disrupting critical services.

Classical methods rely on idealized propagation models and degrade under real-world RF conditions with noise and multipath effects.

Our Solution: Reformulate localization as an inductive graph regression task and propose CAGE (Confidence-guided Adaptive Global Estimation), which fuses a GNN predictor with a domain prior (WCL) via a learned confidence α.

Key Contributions

1First GNN-based approach to jamming localization; robust under sparse/obfuscated RF.
2CAGE confidence-weighted fusion adapts reliance on GNN vs WCL across distance.
3Consistent gains in static & dynamic settings, incl. “outside region” cases.
4Ablations & confidence analysis show full $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CAGE}}$ (with supernode/MLP weighting) yields best accuracy and distance-aware $\alpha$.

Method

CAGE architecture: Graph construction → GNN encoder → Adaptive fusion.

Loss Function

Encouraging independent learning of the GNN regressor. Optimizing only the adaptive loss risks turning the GNN into a residual corrector for WCL. We adopt a joint loss:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{CAGE}} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mathcal{L}_{\text{GNN}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Adapt}}\right) + \lambda \sum_{m \in \mathcal{B}} \left(1 - \boldsymbol{\alpha}^{(m)}\right)^2 \] \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{GNN}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{m \in \mathcal{B}} \left\| \hat{\mathbf{x}}_j^{(m)} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{GNN}}^{(m)} \right\|^2 \] \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{Adapt}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{m \in \mathcal{B}} \Big\| \hat{\mathbf{x}}_j^{(m)} - \big( \boldsymbol{\alpha}^{(m)} \odot \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{GNN}}^{(m)} + ( \mathbf{1} - \boldsymbol{\alpha}^{(m)} ) \odot \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{WCL}}^{(m)} \big) \Big\|^2 \]

where $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_j^{(m)}$ is the ground-truth jammer position in a normalized angular representation, $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{GNN}}^{(m)}$ is the GNN’s predicted position, and $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{WCL}}^{(m)}$ is the WCL estimate. The confidence vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{(m)} \in \mathbb{R}^5$, learned from the supernode representation, dynamically balances the contribution of GNN and WCL via element-wise weighting ($\odot$).

$\mathcal{L}_{\text{GNN}}$ trains the GNN to predict the jammer’s position directly; $\mathcal{L}_{\text{Adapt}}$ optimizes the weighted fusion of GNN and WCL; the final regularizer discourages over-reliance on WCL by nudging $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ toward 1. We set $\lambda = 0$ in experiments, retaining the term for flexibility.

Experimental Results

We evaluate CAGE in two settings. Static: fixed receiver layouts with the jammer placed either inside or outside the sampled region to stress extrapolation beyond measured signals. Dynamic: a mobile receiver approaches and encircles the jammer, enabling analysis of accuracy across distance bins and how performance improves as spatially diverse measurements accumulate.

Confidence Weighting (α)

How we train CAGE determines when it trusts GNN predictions vs the WCL prior. We compare training with only the adaptive loss against the full joint loss. On the encircling trajectory, broad spatial context at long range favors the GNN, while dense, radially symmetric sampling near the jammer favors WCL, so α should start high and gradually shift toward WCL as distance shrinks.

Training with LAdapt alone yields low α across distances (over-reliance on WCL).
With full LCAGE, α is high at long range (trust GNN) and shifts toward WCL near the jammer.
Takeaway: LCAGE learns a distance-aware fusion, avoiding over-reliance on WCL and improving robustness.

Conclusions

Summary

CAGE delivers consistent gains in both static and dynamic localization, including challenging “outside region” cases.

Why It Works

Confidence-weighted fusion adapts to distance, leveraging learned spatial structure without abandoning domain priors.

Next Steps

Temporal GNN architectures, cross-domain generalization, and real-world deployment.
